one of the most common genetic disorders known

CF is caused by mutations in a gene that produces a protein called CFTR. The CFTR protein controls the flow of salt and water in and out of the cells of organs like the lungs and pancreas.

People who have CF inherit two copies of the defective CF gene — one from each parent. People with only one copy of the defective CF gene are called carriers, but they do not have the disease themselves.

cf is an inherited disease

of the secretory glands.

These include glands that make mucus and sweat. If you have CF, your mucus becomes thick and sticky. It builds up in your lungs and blocks your airways. The buildup of mucus makes it easy for bacteria to grow. This leads to repeated, serious lung infections. Over time, these infections can severely damage your lungs.

    symptoms include:
  • Very salty-tasting skin
  • Persistent coughing
  • Frequent lung infections
  • Wheezing or shortness of breath
  • Poor growth or slow weight gain

there is hope on the horizon

In 2012, the FDA approved the groundbreaking drug Kalydeco™, the first drug to treat the underlying cause of CF, not just the symptoms. It only affects one particular type of CF mutation, approximately 5% of CF patients.

But a new treatment approach is in development to combine Kalydeco with another drug, Lumacaftor, which is currently in its final clinical trial stage. This treatment would affect nearly 50% of the CF population.

we need your help

The Kalydeco + Lumacaftor trials are the latest example of the CFF’s innovative venture philanthropy model, in which The Foundation raises and invests millions of dollars in research toward new treatments.

Kalydeco and luvmacaftor were discovered by Vertex Pharmaceuticals in collaboration with the CFF, which provided substantial clinical and financial support in the development process, including approximately $75 million in research funding. The Foundation has since committed another $75 million for the discovery and development of additional CF drugs, like lumacaftor.
